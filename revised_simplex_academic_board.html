The Revised Simplex Method

A compact academic synthesis of the revised simplex method, from standard-form formulation and pricing to pivot selection, degeneracy handling, and the three final outcomes of the algorithm.

1

The Problem in Standard Form

The revised simplex method operates on a linear program written in a form compatible with the algorithm: equality constraints and nonnegative variables.

minimize f = cTx    subject to Ax = b,  x ≥ 0

Constraints of type ≤ introduce slack variables. Constraints of type ≥ introduce surplus variables and, when needed, artificial variables in order to construct an admissible initial basis.

Big-M principle

Artificial variables are penalized in the objective function through a term of the form M · Σaart, so that the algorithm drives them out of the final solution. In the source presentation, this barrier is scaled dynamically with respect to the magnitude of the real coefficients.

2

Basic Matrix Structure

The constraint matrix is decomposed into basic and nonbasic columns. This separation is the essence of the revised method, because it enables efficient updates without constructing the full simplex tableau.

A = [B   N],    x = [xB, xN]T,    xB = B−1b
What is maintained

the current basis B, the basic solution xB, the reduced costs, and the transformed column associated with the entering variable.

Why it is efficient

it works with linear systems and factorizations of B, rather than updating every row and column of the classical simplex tableau.

3

Simplex Multipliers and Reduced Costs

Dual information is extracted from the current basis. The simplex multipliers represent the implicit prices of the constraints, while the reduced costs indicate whether a nonbasic variable can improve the objective.

BTπ = cB   ⇔   πT = cBTB−1
rj = πTaj − cj

In the sign convention adopted here, the current solution is optimal if rj ≤ 0 for every nonbasic variable. If some rs > 0, then the column as is selected to enter the basis.

4

The Iterative Cycle of the Revised Method

1
Standardizationtransform the constraints into equalities and construct a feasible initial basis.
2
Pricingcompute multipliers π and reduced costs r; select the entering variable.
3
Search directionsolve Bd = as in order to determine how the basic solution changes.
4
Minimum ratio testdetermine the largest admissible step that preserves feasibility and identify the leaving variable.
5
Basis updatereplace the leaving column with the entering one and repeat the cycle until a final stopping condition is met.
5

Search Direction and the Leaving Rule

Bd = as   ⇒   d = B−1as

The vector d describes how the basic variables change when the entering variable increases from zero. In order to preserve feasibility, the step length is limited by the minimum ratio test.

θ* = min { (xB)i / di  |  di > 0 }

The row attaining the minimum determines which variable leaves the basis. In this way, the new solution remains inside the feasible region while moving in a direction that improves the objective.

6

Degeneracy and the Lexicographic Rule

In degenerate situations, several rows may yield the same minimum ratio. An arbitrary choice can lead to cycling, meaning that the algorithm revisits the same basis without genuine progress.

Charnes' anti-cycling safeguard

Candidate rows are compared in standardized lexicographic order. The rule enforces a deterministic pivot choice and prevents cycling.

In practice, this places mathematical consistency above arbitrary tie-breaking and makes the implementation more robust in boundary cases and under numerical stress.

7

Updating the Basis and the Objective

Once the entering and leaving variables have been selected, the basis is updated and the solution together with the dual indicators is recomputed for the next iteration.

xBnew = xB − θ*d,    xsnew = θ*
fnew = f − θ*rs

With this sign convention, rs > 0 produces a controlled decrease of the objective value in a minimization problem.

8

Why “Revised” Instead of a Full Tableau?

  • It reduces computational cost by avoiding the full simplex tableau at every iteration.
  • It improves numerical stability through factorizations and linear-system solves applied only to the current basis.
  • It offers transparency with respect to the key quantities: B, xB, π, r, and d.
  • It is well suited to large-scale problems and to implementations that need to expose each iteration clearly.
9

The Three Final Outcomes

The iterative process converges to exactly one of the situations below. These outcomes close the algorithm formally and provide the final mathematical interpretation of the problem.

Optimal Solution

All reduced costs satisfy the optimality criterion and all artificial variables are zero. The original problem is feasible and the optimum has been reached.

Infeasibility

The optimality criterion is satisfied, yet at least one artificial variable remains strictly positive. The conclusion is that the original system has no feasible solution.

Infinite Optimum / Unbounded

There exists an entering variable, but the search direction allows unlimited improvement without violating the constraints. The objective function is not bounded.
